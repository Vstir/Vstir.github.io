About

With aims to promote increased creativity and innovation, the intersection of art and digital design involves using digital tools and technologies to transform physical spaces into immersive, interactive and visually stunning experiences (Liza, 2023).

Art installations are one of the many fields born from this intersection, encompassing a variety of media forms (Tate, 2017). These are artistic creations that push the limits of creative expression and engage viewers on both a visceral and cerebral level to sink in escapism (Lorenzin, 2021).

Digital design acts as the catalyst for pushing overall experience for both artists and users. It aids in transcending traditional constraints by skilfully using multimedia features like interactive displays, projections, and augmented reality (Liza, 2023). The synergy of digital design and art installations allows for a multi-sensory experience in which viewers take part in the story as it develops rather than just watching it happen (Chen, 2018) . The construction of installations that mix the virtual and the real is made possible by the merging of the tangible and intangible with digital design (Lansroth, 2016).

Showcase

Yayoi Kusama: Infinity Mirrors

Yayoi Kusuma caught the imagination of her audiences with her first infinity mirror room - Phalli’s Field, which references her obsession for polka dots (Campbell, 2020). She then went on to make several unique infinity chambers that offer a chance to consider the artist's main themes, which include the celebration of life and its aftermath (HIRSHHORN, 2009).

Each of Kusama's kaleidoscopic environments, which range from multimedia installations to peep-show-like chambers, gives visitors the opportunity to enter an illusion of endless space. The intention was to create a perceptual experience out of the strong repetition found in her previous paintings and works on paper (HIRSHHORN, 2009). With the magic of mirrors Kusuma found work to be less physically taxing and was able to trick the senses and create poetic art installations that speak to our most human responses, creating environments for existential contemplation (Campbell, 2020).

Olafur Eliasson: The weather Project

The topic of the weather has long influenced what people talk about in casual settings. Eliasson used this ubiquitous topic as the foundation for his investigation into concepts related to experience, meditation, and representation. (Tate, 2003).

The site-specific installation is a large semi-circle suspended from a mirrored ceiling, which the reflection from the light created gave the appearance of a full circle. The mirrors on the ceiling produced the image of the space below that was visible, this gives the impression of being close to the sun (Public Delivery, 2019). The intention was for the audience to appear minuscule in comparison, this connotes an extraordinary spectacle as the space makes humans more aware of their humanity and how insignificant we are in the scope of the solar system. For Eliasson this installation was not entertainment, the focus was for humans to feel and think of the meaning of their lives (Public Delivery, 2019).

Random International: Rain Room

Random International's Rain Room (2012) is an immersive space with water pouring continuously and pausing once a human is detected, aiming to engage viewers by examining behaviour and natural events to challenge the human experience in a world driven by machines. Rain Room may be viewed as a magnified depiction of our surroundings, the installation creates a distinct mood by investigating how human connections with one another and nature are increasingly mediated by technology (RANDOM INTERNATIONAL, 2012).

Visitors will have the chance to experience something that seems unattainable: controlling the rain. The Rain Room offers a break from daily living as well as a chance for sensory introspection in a supportive environment (LACMA, n.d.).
